## Session 1 — Introduction

### What is a small business?
- **Criteria for "small":**
  - Number of employees < 15 (Fair Work Commission).
  - Low sales revenue: < $2 million (ATO) or < $50 million (APRA – Australian Prudential Regulation Authority).
  - Small impact on its industry.
- **Definition used in this unit:** a business that is **independently owned, operated and financed**, has **fewer than 100 employees**, and has **relatively little impact** on its industry.
- **Ways to define a small business:** number of employees, sales revenue, total value of assets, value of the owner's equity.
- **What qualifies as a business:** any commercial activity (e.g., a stay-home parent providing day care; ownership of a fast-food restaurant of a chain).

### Small business sectors
- **Services:** 32% of the economy and 41% of the workforce (2018-19, ASBFEO). Example: **Geek Squad Inc.** (started by Robert Stephens as a single-person firm in 1994, merged with Best Buy in 2002).
- **Manufacturing:** small-business advantage over large firms = **product customisation** (artwork, jewelry, clothing, furniture).
- **High Technology:** businesses depending heavily on advanced scientific and engineering knowledge; 39% of high-tech jobs are in small business.
- **IT Business (focus of the unit):** IT consultancy (cyber security, IT support, business analytics, machine automation), website/app development, software development, networking services, computer repair, online retail (e-tailing / e-Commerce).

### Why target small business?
- "Bigger is not necessarily better": reduced corporate taxes, more personal service (services sector), larger firms have less agility and less control over decision-making.
- Increased business start-ups: small business growth more than doubled in 30 years.
- Increasing interest at colleges and universities (US: 16 schools in 1971 → more than 2,000 in 2010).

### Economics (key concepts)
- **Economics:** study of how people allocate **scarce resources** for production, distribution and consumption.
- **Economising problem:** satisfying unlimited human wants from a limited number of resources.
- **Microeconomics:** behavior of the individual producer and consumer and allocation of limited resources (**subject matter of this unit**).
- **Macroeconomics:** a nation's total economic behavior (e.g., inflation rate).

### Innovation & Entrepreneurship
- Innovation and entrepreneurship are a **source of business opportunity**.
- Australia ranks in the middle third of OECD countries on most innovation indicators (Australian Innovation System Report 2013).
- **Small businesses account for 55% of all innovations.** Examples: audio tape recorder, double-knit fabric, fiber-optic examining equipment, heart valve, optical scanner, personal computer, soft contact lenses, zipper, airplane.
- **Better decision making:** in Victoria ~240 new businesses open each week; some fail, some thrive. Example: **Dick Smith** nearly failed (had $18,000 of stock stolen) yet became one of Australia's most successful businesspeople.
- **Secrets of success:** big businesses need small businesses (e.g., Ford supplied by smaller businesses); small businesses are more flexible/agile; competitive advantage; close relationship with the customer.

### Risks of small business ownership
- **Business failure:** situation where a business is unable to sustain its operations or achieve its objectives, leading to closure.
- **Causes of failure:** inadequate management, inadequate financing, inability to compete, defective planning.
- **Business termination ≠ failure:** simply winding down the business due to dissolving a partnership, court order, or death of the sole trader.

### How to create a successful small business
- **Motivation:** the owner must always have the will to succeed; they need drive, determination and the ability to recover from mistakes. Motives: desire for independence, to make a profit, and to fulfil a market need. Requires courage, determination and stamina.
- "Think big": have confidence and motivation to change the world.
- **Important issues in new venture creation:**
  - Does the entrepreneur have effective ideas and the courage to give them a chance?
  - Is the entrepreneur prepared to master strategy and competitive advantage?
  - Can they identify a market niche being missed by other firms?
  - Can they identify a new market not yet discovered?
  - Can they generate **first-mover advantage** by exploiting a niche or entering a market before competitors?
- **Questions that keep a new venture focused on its customers:**
  - Who is your customer?
  - How will you reach key customer market segments?
  - What determines customer choices to buy or not buy?
  - Why is your product/service a compelling choice?
  - How will you price it?
  - How much does it cost to make and deliver it?
  - How much does it cost to attract a customer?
  - How much does it cost to support and retain a customer?

## Session 2 — Entrepreneurship & Forms of Ownership

### What is Entrepreneurship?
- **Entrepreneur:** someone who "takes advantage of a business opportunity by assuming the financial, material and psychological risks of starting a new company." French word from the 17th century, translating to "go-between."
- **Entrepreneurship:** the process of designing, launching and running a new business, which typically begins as a small business (e.g., a startup) offering a product, process or service for sale or hire. Most economists agree it is a necessary ingredient for stimulating economic growth and employment.
- **Entrepreneurial behaviours (5):**
  1. **Creation** – a new business is started.
  2. **Innovation** – the business involves a new idea.
  3. **Risk assumption** – the owner bears the risk of loss.
  4. **General management** – the owner guides the business.
  5. **Performance intention** – the owner expects a high level of growth/profit.
- **Who are the entrepreneurs? (traits):** internal locus of control, high energy level, self-control, tolerance for ambiguity, self-reliance and desire for independence, high need for achievement, flexibility, passion and action orientation.
- **8 traits of a successful entrepreneur (Forbes):** Determination, Risk-Taking (calculated risks), Optimism, Curiosity, Problem-Solving, Confidence, Adaptability to Change, Persistence.

### Entrepreneur success factors
- **Rewards of going into business for yourself:** opportunity for independence, outlet for creativity, opportunity for a better lifestyle, opportunity for profit.
- **Risks:** personal liability, long working hours, limited and uncertain income, fewer places to seek advice.
- **Entrepreneur vs. Small Business Manager:**
  - **Entrepreneur:** identifies opportunities to create a new business for unique needs, initiates a start-up process, is a **leader**.
  - **Small Business Manager:** looks after an ongoing process of owning/operating an established business, focuses on running it (may not require a start-up process), is a **manager**.
- **Five bad reasons to become an entrepreneur (Forbes):** because you really need a job; want to work less; are sick of working for someone; want to become rich; want to become famous.

### Forms of Business Organisations
There is **no single best form of ownership** — it depends on many factors. Main options: **Sole Proprietorship, Partnership, Company/Corporation** (plus Cooperative and Trust).

**Sole Trader / Sole Proprietorship**
- A business owned and operated by one person; no cumbersome legal requirements to establish.
- Only specific legal requirement: register the business name with **ASIC**, but only if it differs from the owner's name.
- **Taxation:** use individual **TFN** (Tax File Number); apply for an **ABN**; register for **GST** if annual GST turnover ≥ $75,000; pay tax at individual income tax rates; pay quarterly **PAYG** instalments; can claim a deduction for personal super contributions.

**Partnership**
- An **unincorporated** structure (not registered as legally separate from owners → owners are liable).
- **Minimum 2, maximum 20 partners.** Exceptions: medical practitioners & stockbrokers (up to 50); veterinarians, architects & chemists (up to 100); solicitors & accountants (up to 400).
- **Articles of Partnership** cover: name, location, purpose; each partner's contribution (cash, services, property); authority of each partner and consensual decision-making; management responsibilities; duration; division of profits/losses; salaries/draws (a **draw** = money a partner takes out of the business from profit).
- **Taxation:** the firm pays no tax — individual partners pay taxes. Income, losses and control are shared. The partnership has its own **TFN** and **ABN**, must lodge an annual partnership return, and must register for GST if turnover ≥ $75,000.
- **Uniform Partnership Act (UPA, US):** three key elements of a general partnership = (1) common ownership, (2) shared profits and losses, (3) the right to participate in managing operations.
- **Limited partnership:** liability of one or more partners is limited. Consists of general partners (unlimited liability) and limited partners (liability limited to their investment; no maximum number). An **incorporated limited partnership** is used for high-risk venture capital projects. Limited/incorporated limited partnerships must be registered with **Consumer Affairs Victoria (CAV)**.

**Company / Corporation**
- **Incorporation:** changing ownership structure to a company. Requires registering the company name with **ASIC**, which issues a certificate of incorporation and an **ACN** (Australian Company Number). Directors are appointed to run the company; once incorporated the company has a **separate legal identity** from its owners, now called **shareholders/members**.
- **Corporation:** most complicated business structure; "an autonomous entity that has the legal rights of a person including the ability to sue and be sued, to own property, and to engage in business transactions." Must act per its charter and state laws.
- **Taxation:** must lodge an annual company tax return; **no tax-free threshold**; pays income tax on profits at the company tax rate; must pay superannuation for eligible workers (including directors).
- **Proprietary (private) company:** most common type in Australia; usually 2–50 private shareholders; must include **"Proprietary Limited" (Pty Ltd)** after its name.
- **Public corporation:** sells shares to the public and is listed on a stock exchange (ASX). Subject to more rigorous federal/state/ASIC regulations, such as disclosing financial info in the annual report (e.g., CBA, ANZ, BHP, Telstra, Qantas, Coles, Woolworths, Westpac). Needs **ACN + ABN**.
- **S Corporation (US):** a closely held corporation electing to be taxed under Subchapter S; generally pays no income taxes.

**Cooperative**
- Formed when individuals and/or organisations combine for a common purpose; a democratic organisation owned and controlled by the people it serves (e.g., farmers' cooperative, community education centre, building societies, credit unions).
- **Features:** registered in Victoria under the **Co-operatives Act 1996**; **minimum 7 members**; no member may hold more than **20% of shares**; a legal entity managed by a board of directors; profits can be reinvested rather than paid to shareholders (if a majority agrees).

**Case study:** "How to choose the right business structure for success" (Bean Ninjas) — there is no one-size-fits-all structure; seek professional advice.

## Session 3 — Franchising

### About franchising
- In existence since the **early 1800s** (agents selling reapers and sewing machines); evolved past exclusive use of agents; considerable growth since the **1950s**.
- Today franchising is represented in almost every industry, with growing interest in **international franchising**.

### Franchising systems (two main forms)
- **Product-distribution franchising:** a manufacturer authorizes retailers to sell a certain brand-name item; a producer licenses distributors to sell a product to retailers. (Focus is on selling the product.)
- **Business-format franchising:** the franchisor supplies brand names, techniques, or services instead of a complete product. A **turnkey approach** — the franchisee buys the franchisor's product plus the **entire way of doing business** (operating procedures, marketing packages, physical building and equipment, full business services). Used in quick-service restaurants, lodging, retail food, and table/full-service restaurants (e.g., hotel/motel chains, **McDonald's**).

### 4 types of franchises
1. **Single-unit franchise** – one location.
2. **Multi-unit franchise** – several locations.
3. **Franchise Area Developer** – rights to develop an area.
4. **Master / regional franchising** – rights over a whole region.

### Advantages
- **To the franchisor:** fast and well-controlled distribution of its products; no need to construct and operate its own outlets; more working capital available for expanded production and advertising; franchising agreements maintain product and quality standards; motivated work force (franchisees share profits).
- **To the franchisee:** opportunity to start a **proven business with limited capital**; guaranteed customers; franchisor available for advice and guidance; materials for local promotional campaigns plus participation in national campaigns; cost savings from purchasing in cooperation with other franchisees.

### Disadvantages
- **To the franchisor:** failure of the franchisee to operate the franchise properly; disputes/lawsuits by franchisees over the terms of the franchise.
- **To the franchisee:** franchisor retains a large amount of control over the franchisee's activities; franchisor may open competing franchises within the franchisee's market.

### Evaluating a potential franchise
- **Get franchise information:** name, address, description, number of franchising units, franchising fee, royalty fee, capital requirements, etc. Also talk to people currently running one — ask what it costs to operate monthly, how long to break even, how profitable it is.
- **Analyse the market:** is there demand for the product/service in your territory at your prices? Will the population grow/stay/decline over 5 years? Will demand rise/stay/fall in 5 years? What competition (franchise and non-franchise firms) already exists?
- **Get disclosure statements:** information franchisors are required to provide to potential franchisees.
- **The Franchise Agreement:** the legal contract binding both parties; defines the precise, detailed conditions of the relationship. May or may not be negotiable. **Consult a lawyer and a CPA (Certified Practising Accountant) before signing.**
- **Fees:**
  - **Franchise fee** – amount paid to become a franchisee.
  - **Royalty fees** – usually a percentage of **gross sales** (not profits) paid to the franchisor.
  - **Advertising fees** – ensure a portion goes to local advertising in your area.
- **Key agreement terms to check:** termination, terms and renewal, exclusive territory. Do not assume the disclosure statement tells you everything (see ACCC Franchising Code of Conduct).
- **Australian info sources:** franchisebusiness.com.au (best source); franchisingexpo.com.au (annual exhibition); topfranchise.com ratings.

### International franchising
- Overseas franchising becomes more attractive as domestic competition increases in saturated local markets.
- **95% of the world's consumers live outside the U.S. and Australia**, and foreign consumers already know U.S./Australian products.

### Case study — Pizza Hut
- Uses the Pizza Hut Franchise Information Booklet, covering: History, The Process, Support, Investment, and FAQs.

## Session 4 — Starting a Business

Covers the full path to launch a small business in Victoria: **steps → checklist → business plan → costing → compliance → lessons learnt**.

### Checklist for starting a business
- **Do market research.**
- **Choose the right business structure** (links to Session 2 forms of ownership; there's a step-by-step tool to pick the best structure).

### Business Plan — writing it
- **Set your vision** and draw up a **vision statement** of where you want the business to be; it guides what you need to achieve. Use it to "brain dump" ideas, possibilities and potential problems.
- **Brainstorm three top strategies** you can practically implement to meet the vision.
- For each strategy, run a **force field analysis** (factors that will make or break the strategy) and draw up **three action points**.

### Purpose of a Business Plan
- **Attract capital** for start-up and **provide direction** after start-up.
- Should tell the reader: **What, When, Where, Why, How, and Who.**

### Business Plan contents
Cover page; Table of contents; **Executive summary** (company info, market opportunity, financial data — max ~2 pages, a one-to-two-page overview of the whole plan); Background; Environment & industry analysis; Products/services; Marketing research & evaluation; Manufacturing & operations plan; Management team; Timeline; Critical risks & assumptions; Benefit to community; Exit strategy; Financial plan; Appendix.

Key detail per section:
- **Cover page:** business name, address, phone, date issued.
- **Background:** legal business form, reasons for establishment, company history.
- **Environmental analysis:** national/international trends and changes affecting the business — economic, competitive, legal, political, technological arenas. **Industry analysis** describes the industry; explain how your product/service differs from what's on the market.
- **Marketing research & evaluation:** present evidence a market exists (size and nature); what buyers have in common (segment markets, e.g., by demographics such as 18–25-year-old females); identify 3–4 primary competitors — the **price leader, quality leader, and service leader**; explain how you'll assess customer needs over time.
- **Marketing plan** (how you'll hit the sales forecast) built on the marketing mix: **Pricing** (one of the most important decisions), **Promotion** (attract/communicate with customers), **Place** (own sales force vs. independent reps/distributors), **Service policies** (service and warranty).
- **Manufacturing & operations plan:** facilities, location/space, capital equipment, labour force, inventory control, purchasing.
- **Management team:** the key to turning vision into success — must be comprehensive in **technical skills, business skills, and experience**.
- **Timeline:** interrelationships and timing of major events.
- **Critical risks & assumptions:** e.g., unreliable sales forecasts, unfavourable industry-wide trends, competitors underpricing, trained workers not available, erratic supply of products/raw materials.
- **Benefit to community**, and **Exit strategy** (sell in 20 years? children take over?).

### Financial Plan contents
Existing businesses need historical statements + pro forma projections; start-ups have only projections:
- Sources and uses of capital (initial and projected).
- **Cash flow projections** for three years.
- **Balance sheets** for three years.
- **Profit-and-loss statements** for three years.
- **Break-even analysis.**

### Costing — estimate your start-up costs
Break costs into:
- **One-off / establishment costs** (e.g., licence fees, insurance).
- **Cost of purchasing necessary equipment/assets** (equipment, tools) for the next few years.
- **Working capital:** money for operations, opportunities/growth, and to cover unexpected financial costs.

**Common start-up costs:** business premises (site design/architectural plan, premise modifications — electrical, lighting, painting, security, ventilation, kitchen fit-out, bathroom/plumbing); **staff** (recruitment, wages/salaries, superannuation, uniforms); **professional services** (legal, accountancy, debt collection, business coaching); **running costs** (wages, buying stock, internet fees, shipping/delivery, rent, utilities); **marketing**.

### Key financial statements (definitions)
- **Cash Flow Statement (CFS):** summarizes cash and cash equivalents entering and leaving a company; shows financial health, operational efficiency and how well it generates/manages cash.
- **Balance Sheet:** reports a company's **assets, liabilities and shareholder equity** — a snapshot of what it owns and owes plus what shareholders invested.
- **Profit and Loss (P&L) Statement:** summarizes revenues, costs, expenses and profits/losses over a period — shows ability to generate revenue, manage costs and make profits. *(In the assignment's Business Proposal Report this is called the **Income Statement**.)*
- **Break-Even Analysis:** compares sales income to fixed costs; the **break-even point (BEP)** is the sales amount needed to cover all fixed costs before profit begins (can be expressed in product units or sales dollars).

### Compliance
- Beyond registering the **business, ABN and GST**, many **licences** apply:
  - **Permits:** council permits, commercial vehicle parking, electrical work training, rehabilitation, etc.
  - **Certificates:** food handling, Responsible Serving of Alcohol, etc.
  - **Insurance:** public liability, professional indemnity, building, contents, income, WorkSafe, etc.
- Find licences via the **Australian Business Licence and Information Service (ABLIS)** — ablis.business.gov.au.

### Lessons learnt
- Detailed and **systematic planning** is required; the more you plan, the more chances of success. The business plan is a progressive, step-by-step process. *"If you fail to plan, you plan to fail."*

### Preparing the presentation pitch
Tell a **real customer story** (show the problem); state **market size** (why it matters); outline your **business model**; make the presentation crystal clear; **talk about yourself/team**; show **progress** (what you've done lately); address **competition** head-on; give the **numbers behind your numbers** (finance); and **show your product** (Minimum Viable Product + what makes it unique).

## Session 5 — Social Media Marketing and Digital Marketing

### Evolution of marketing
Traditional Marketing → Mobile Marketing → E-Commerce / Digital Marketing → Social Media Marketing. *(Note in the slides: Dr. Raikundalia questions the order, believing E-Commerce/Digital preceded Mobile Marketing.)*

### Traditional marketing
- Facilitated only **one-way communication**; audiences could not easily respond, and not in real time.
- Channels: **television, radio, newspaper, billboard.**

### Mobile marketing
- **Definition:** a multi-channel digital marketing strategy aimed at reaching a target audience on their **smartphones, tablets and other mobile devices**, using mobile-optimized ads, apps, SMS, email and websites in a personalized, immediate way. Most mobile activity is on **social networks and messaging apps**.
- **Key stats:** 65% of all email is first opened on mobile; 48% of mobile internet sessions start on a search engine; 56% of B2B buyers frequently use smartphones to access vendors' content; 95% of adults primarily use smartphones to access content/information (Marketo Australia).

### Mobile optimisation
- Sites must be **mobile-optimised to rank on Google** (or be penalised). Check that: content loads quickly (people abandon slow sites), content fits the screen without zooming, the site returns no mobile errors, mobile content engages the audience, and whether pages need more optimisation.

### SMS marketing
- Sending messages directly to customers'/prospects' mobile devices.
- **Stats:** 98% of SMS is opened (vs. email); 45% response rate to SMS vs. 8% for email; ~17 billion mobile devices in use (2024); ~1 in 5 commercial emails ends up junked/blocked.
- **Succeeding with SMS:** keep to **160 characters or less**; offer something of value; make clear who is sending; use the users' language (avoid slang/abbreviations unless a young group); communicate clearly. Be **targeted, relevant, timely and personalised.**

### What is digital marketing?
- Marketing products/services for increased sales and brand awareness via **electronic media** (computers, laptops, tablets, smartphones, the Internet) — using digital technology to market mostly across the Internet.

### Website content strategy
- Before writing a page, define its **primary goal** and target reader: What is their problem? What's in it for them? Where are they in the buying process? What keywords/phrases would they search? The best writers **put themselves in the buyer's shoes** and write like a one-on-one conversation.
- **Key hints:** create high-quality content that appeals to the target audience; match the audience's literacy level; don't rush content — **voice and tone matter.**
- **Questions to answer:** Why (are you taking on this project)? What (is the message)? Where (will they read it — location/device/context)? When (timing to create and publish)? How (structure/present the content)?

### Activities to ensure (usability & readability)
- Offer people what they're looking for; let users find things quickly (or they leave); put the **most important information first**; keep it simple (don't be overly clever).
- **Write for scanners:** headlines that say what it's about; visuals that capture the message (replace text with pictures/videos where relevant); sub-headings that summarise key points; bullet points to shorten paragraphs.
- **Easy-to-read content:** short sentences (≤12 words); skip unnecessary words/jargon; short paragraphs (max 3–4 sentences); avoid repetition; use **active voice**; address the audience directly using "**you**".

### What do we want to achieve? (digital marketing goals)
- Goals can include: improving reach/website traffic, brand awareness, audience engagement, lead generation/nurturing, increasing marketing **ROI**, or customer retention and loyalty. (E.g., brand awareness via sponsorship or influencer reviews.)
- **Target audience:** use data and insights (own or third-party) to determine the content types to use.
- **Content ownership:** if you lack in-house resources, use a media agency (type depends on budget/content); always consider **licensing and ownership** — decide if you need to own the rights.

### What is social media marketing?
- **Social media:** Internet-based applications built on the ideological and technological foundations of **Web 2.0**, allowing creation and exchange of **user-generated content**.
- **Content forms:** text, videos, pictures, networks — most popular forms: blogs, microblogs, social networks.
- **Engagement now:** businesses moved from one-way communication to a **value-exchange model** (mutual dialogue and benefit sharing between company and consumer); digital lets companies attract new customers and reach existing ones more efficiently.
- **Level of control:** you cannot control everything in digital, even though it's easier to measure.

### 10 Principles of Social Media Marketing
1. **Share** – create shareable content with a compelling headline, then publish and promote.
2. **Go real time** – publish topically to catch trends/viral waves; respond to complaints and inquiries in real time.
3. **Educate, don't sell** – inform and solve people's problems with answers and information.
4. **Entertain, don't market** – embrace the "WOW" (competitions, humour, the unexpected) — that's what gets shared.
5. **Go multi-channel** – be on Facebook, Twitter, YouTube and blogs as a minimum, 24/7.
6. **Think multimedia, go visual.**
7. **Think like publishers.**
8. **Target your niche audience.**
9. **Listen to your audience.**
10. **Engage.**

## Session 6 — E-Commerce: Doing Small Business on the Internet

### Disintermediation
- **Intermediary:** an agent, software or business providing a trading infrastructure that brings buyers and sellers together.
- **Disintermediation:** eliminating the intermediary between buyer and seller. **Reasons:** save on commission costs; more efficient delivery (e.g., buying a ticket directly from the airline rather than a travel agency).
- The Internet has impacted almost every industry: travel, entertainment, electronics, financial services, retail, automobiles, education/training.

### Evolution of the Web
- **Web 1.0 (1991–2003):** static sites authored only by "webmasters"; everyday people couldn't add/edit content.
- **Web 2.0:** the next generation — a mature communications platform characterized by **collaboration, free, and sharing**; content sharing through open sourcing, user-contributed content, and collaboration inside and outside the organization.
- **Web 3.0:** "intelligent" web apps using natural language processing, machine learning and reasoning. **Semantic Web:** organises information and supports search based on the **meaning** of words/terms; integration of legacy devices, open technologies, worldwide online database.
- **E-commerce:** buying and selling of goods/services over the Internet. **E-business:** includes e-commerce plus all activities related to internal and external business operations.

### Marketplaces vs. E-marketplaces
- **Marketplace:** a physical location where goods/services are exchanged (e.g., the traditional market square).
- **E-marketplace:** an electronic exchange where firms register as sellers or buyers to conduct business over the Internet. **Components:** customers, sellers, goods, infrastructure, a front end, a back end, and support services.

### What is E-Commerce?
- Doing business online (via the Web) — digitally-enabled commercial transactions between/among organizations and individuals.
- **Total worldwide e-commerce revenue in 2023: US$3.6 trillion.**
- **The first e-commerce?** In 1886 a telegraph operator sold refused watches to fellow operators via telegraph, earning enough to start his own store — **Richard Sears** (later Sears, Roebuck and Company).

### Important features of e-commerce
- **Mass customization:** tailoring products/services to customer specifications (e.g., pre-fabricated housing).
- **Personalization:** knowing enough about a customer's preferences to make more appealing offers (e.g., Tesla driver profiles).
- **Round-the-clock (24/7) service.**
- **Extended distribution channels:** reaching customers who otherwise wouldn't come.
- **Reduced transaction costs** (searching for buyers/sellers, etc.).

### M-business / M-commerce
- **Mobile business:** purchasing goods/services through a wireless, Internet-enabled device.
- **M-commerce:** electronic commerce through mobile devices — used for products/services, location-based services (maps), video/audio content, football scores via SMS, information-on-demand (news), banking/stock brokerage via SMS/WAP/iMode.
- Term coined in the late 1990s (dot-com boom); expectations around 2.5G/3G drove hundreds of billions in UMTS/3G licensing fees (2000–2001).
- **Advantages:** convenient on-the-go purchasing from any location, customer satisfaction, cost savings, new business opportunities.

### EC dimensions (three dimensions: product, process, delivery agent)
- **Traditional commerce:** all three dimensions are **physical** — brick-and-mortar / old-economy organizations selling physical products offline via physical agents.
- **Pure Play EC:** all three dimensions **digital** — pure online (virtual), new-economy organizations selling only online.
- **Partial EC:** a mix of digital and physical — **click-and-mortar** organizations that do their primary business physically but conduct some EC.

### EC models (by function)
- **Virtual storefront:** sells goods/services online.
- **Online marketplace.**
- **Information broker:** collects and compiles data for organisations.
- **Transaction broker:** neutral specialist (e.g., real estate) supporting buyers and sellers.
- **E-business model:** a plan detailing how a company creates, delivers and generates revenue on the Internet.

### Classification of EC by nature of the transaction
- **B2B (Business-to-business):** all participants are businesses/organizations.
- **B2C (Business-to-consumer):** businesses sell to individual shoppers.
- **B2B2C:** a business provides product/service to a client business, which serves its own customers (e.g., Airbnb, Amazon, App Stores).
- **C2B (Consumer-to-business):** individuals sell to organizations or seek bidders (e.g., Shutterstock).
- **C2C (Consumer-to-consumer):** consumers sell directly to each other (e.g., eBay).
- **M-commerce:** transactions in a wireless environment.
- **L-commerce (Location-based):** m-commerce targeted to individuals in specific locations at specific times.
- **Intra-business (Organizational) EC:** internal exchanges of goods/services/information among units and individuals.
- **B2E (Business-to-employee):** organization delivers services/info/products to its employees.
- **C-commerce (Collaborative commerce):** individuals/groups communicate or collaborate online to improve efficiency/profits.
- **E-government / G2C:** government buys from or provides goods/services/info to businesses or citizens (e.g., Centrelink).
- **Exchange (electronic):** a public e-market with many buyers and sellers.
- **E2E (Exchange-to-exchange):** exchanges between different businesses' websites (e.g., business ABC's cart interacting with the Stripe payment platform).

### Supply Chain Management (SCM)
- **Supply chain:** network of activities delivering a product/service to the customer — managing the entire chain of raw material supply, assembly and distribution. Framework: **Plan, Source, Make, Deliver** (e.g., Starbucks).
- **Supply chain includes:** sourcing (purchasing) raw materials/parts/inventory, order entry, operations planning, transformation process (manufacturing/services), quality management, and logistics (**transportation/traffic** and **distribution/delivery**).
- **Objectives of SCM:** minimize cost of materials and their movement; minimize inventory investment; ensure timely delivery at every level (availability + speed); ensure quality of materials; get product design/other help from suppliers if needed.
- **Information sharing** aims to **match demand and supply** (what's available, when, from whom): demand data (actual sales, forecasts, booked/custom orders), product availability (inventory, production plans, shipping schedules/shipments), and quality (suppliers' quality data).

### Types of B2B e-commerce websites
- **Electronic storefronts:** buyers consult an online catalog, order, pay/arrange payment, and track shipments (similar to B2C storefronts).
- **Net marketplaces:** buyers and sellers in the same industry negotiate contracts, place orders, track shipments, arrange payment, and collaborate on product design.

### E-Commerce & E-Marketing
- **E-commerce** = buying/selling processes. **E-marketing** = efforts to communicate about, promote and sell products/services over the Internet (a.k.a. web/Internet marketing). Related but different issues.

### Social media for business
- **Social media:** user-generated content and the websites/apps that let people interact and share online — user reviews (Amazon, Yelp), social networks (Facebook, X), crowdsourcing (Amazon Mechanical Turk), blogs (WordPress), photo sharing (Flickr), video sharing (YouTube).
- **Why it matters for businesses:** finds customers/builds clientele; reveals what people say about you and your industry; introduces your brand; gives feedback; provides a test audience (vs. focus groups); solidifies reputation as a valuable, knowledgeable resource.
- **Major questions for small businesses:** How do I use social media? How to incorporate it in my business plan? How do I make money with it? What ROI can I expect? How will it change the way I do business?

### How to start your own online small business (6-step process)
All online businesses go through the **first three steps**: (1) **Create the online content** ("Content is King"), (2) **Host the content** on the Internet, (3) **Market the website and content**. Businesses conducting online sales continue with the **final three**: (4) **Collect and record customer orders**, (5) **Process payments**, (6) **Fulfill customer orders**.

### Building an e-commerce site
- **Key considerations:** **Suppliers** (no products without relevant suppliers); **price point** (price comparisons are easy — important in a transparent market); **customer relations** (email, FAQs, knowledge bases, forums, chat — differentiate from competitors); **the back end** (fulfillment, returns, customer service — these make or break a retailer and define the customer relationship).
- **Other desirable capabilities:** gift sending, affiliate programs, special discounts, repeat-buyer programs, seasonal/periodic sales — all hard to do unless your e-commerce software supports them.

## Session 7 — Small Business Accounting

**Learning objectives:** (1) importance/uses of financial records; (2) accounting records a small business needs; (3) the **11 ratios** used to analyze financial statements; (4) importance and procedures for **managing cash flow**.

### What is small business accounting?
- The system for converting raw data from **source documents** (invoices, sales receipts, bills, checks) into information that helps a manager make business decisions. It "translates numbers — the language of business — into plain English."

### Accounting basics
- Accounting systems revolve around three elements: **Assets** (what the business owns), **Liabilities** (what it owes), and **Owner's Equity** (what the owner has invested — also called capital or net worth).
- **Accounting equation:** Assets = Liabilities + Owner's Equity. (*Disbursement* = paying out money by the business.)

### Double-entry vs. single-entry
- **Double-entry:** every transaction recorded twice — a **debit** to one account and a **credit** to another; every "plus" balanced by a "minus" (assets on one side; liabilities/owner's equity on the other). **Self-checking** — the system must balance.
- **Single-entry:** income and expenses recorded in a running log (like a chequebook). **Not self-checking.** Usable by small sole proprietorships; produces a monthly statement but **cannot** produce a balance sheet, income statement, or other financial records.

### Accounting software
- **QuickBooks Pro** (USA's #1 small business financial software), **MYOB** (Australian; tax/accounting for SMEs), **Xero** (flexible cloud-based accounting software).

### Cash vs. Accrual methods
- **Accrual basis** (used by most businesses): report income/expenses when **earned or incurred**, not when collected/paid. Credit sales recorded as **accounts receivable**; expense payments can be spread over time (e.g., insurance paid once but recorded monthly).
- **Cash basis:** record transactions only when cash is **actually received or paid**. Simpler; suitable for very small businesses, those with no inventory, or cash-only businesses — but can **distort financial results** over time.

### Journals and Ledgers
- Accounting begins by recording raw data (sales slips, purchase invoices, check stubs) in **journals** (date, amounts, accounts to debit/credit). Several journals exist: sales, purchases, cash receipts, cash disbursements.
- At regular intervals, transactions are **posted to the general ledger** — a summary book of all transactions and account balances.

### Core financial statements
- **Income Statement**, **Balance Sheet**, and **Statement of Cash Flow**.

### Using financial statements to run the business
- **Daily:** check cash on hand; check bank balance; calculate daily summaries of sales/cash receipts; note problems in credit collections; record money paid out.
- **Weekly:** update **cash flow** spreadsheet (receipts/disbursements); watch **accounts receivable** (slow payers); note **accounts payable** discounts; calculate **payroll**; track **taxes** (due dates, reports).
  - *Accounts Payable = short-term obligations owed to creditors/suppliers not yet paid; Accounts Receivable = money owed to the business for goods/services delivered but not yet paid.*
- **Monthly:** provide records to any outside accountant; review income statement; review balance sheet; reconcile the business checking account; balance petty cash; review federal tax requirements and make deposits; review and **age** accounts receivable.

### Ratio Analysis (the 11 ratios)
Ratios compare important financial aspects — "profit figures tell only part of the story." **Benchmarking** = comparing a firm's ratios to industry leaders; **Industry average analysis** = comparing to industry averages.

**A) Liquidity ratios** — ability to meet short-term obligations as they come due (liquidity = how quickly an asset becomes cash):
1. **Current Ratio** = current assets ÷ current liabilities. ≤ 1.0 = low / financial difficulty; > 2.0 = very healthy; > 3.0 = may signal inefficient use of current assets.
2. **Quick (Acid-Test) Ratio** — meets short-term obligations with the most liquid assets (**excludes inventory**, which isn't quickly converted to cash). Example: Stereo City had only $0.29 in liquid assets per $1.00 of current liabilities vs. industry average of $0.60 (much less liquid).

**B) Activity ratios** — speed at which assets convert into sales/cash (efficiency of asset use):
3. **Inventory Turnover** — how quickly goods are sold and replenished; higher = selling/turning inventory more times (Stereo City restocked 2.16×/year).
4. **Average Collection Period** — how long to convert a credit sale into cash. High = potential AR issues (delays, poor credit terms); low = efficient AR management, better cash flow, stronger customer relationships.
5. **Fixed Asset Turnover** — how efficiently fixed assets generate sales. Low = ineffective marketing or older equipment/downtime; high = generating sales with relatively few fixed assets.
6. **Total Asset Turnover** — how efficiently **all** assets generate sales; high = good overall management; low = flaws in strategy, poor marketing, or improper capital expenditure.

**C) Leverage ratios** — extent to which the firm uses **debt** for financing and its ability to service that debt (leverage = magnifying risk and potential return using other people's money):
7. **Debt Ratio** — proportion of total assets acquired with borrowed funds (short-term + long-term debt + long-term obligations like leases). High = aggressive, high-risk/high-return; low = conservative / more reliance on equity.
8. **Times Interest Earned Ratio** — ability to meet interest requirements. High = low risk (but possibly inefficient use of leverage); low = act immediately to avoid default.

**D) Profitability ratios** — ability to turn sales into profits and earn profits on committed assets (also insight into management effectiveness):
9. **Net Profit Margin** — percentage of each sales dollar remaining as profit after all expenses (incl. taxes). Low may mean expenses too high relative to sales (varies greatly by industry).
10. **Return on Assets (ROA)** — effectiveness in generating profits from available assets; higher = better management and growth prospects.
11. **Return on Equity (ROE)** — return earned on the owner's investment; generally higher = better, but heavily affected by financial leverage, so may not accurately measure management effectiveness.

### Managing cash flow
- "A company that does not effectively manage its cash flow — by balancing income and expenses day-to-day — is poised for collapse."
- **Accounting definition of cash flow** = net income + noncash expenses (e.g., depreciation and amortization).
- **Three motives for holding cash:** (1) to make **transactions** (primary motive — pay the bills), (2) to protect against unanticipated problems, (3) to invest in opportunities as they arise.
- If a business can't meet its obligations it is **insolvent**; continued insolvency leads directly to **bankruptcy**.

### Cash-to-Cash Cycle
- Also called net operating cycle / cash conversion cycle. The period from when money is **spent on raw materials** until money is **collected on the sale** of the finished good. **Shorter = better** (sales and inventory turnover happening faster).

## Session 8 — Financing: Revenue Models and Pricing

Covers: determining the **financing needs** of your business, **revenue models**, and **pricing models**.

### Initial capital requirements
- **Steps to determine initial capital:** (1) identify short-term and long-term assets and needed expenses; (2) determine the cost and financing for those assets.
- **Sources of financing:** owner-provided funds (**equity**) and borrowed funds (**liabilities/debt**).

### Forms of capital: Debt vs. Equity financing
- **Debt financing:** borrowed funds that must be repaid.
- **Equity funds:** supplied by investors in exchange for an **ownership position**; **need not be repaid** but **reduce the owner's autonomy**.
- **Equity financing:** sale of common stock or use of **retained earnings** (net profit after dividends, reinvested in the business) for long-term financing; **no constraint on the firm's cash flows**.
- **Interests of equity investors:** getting **dividends** (investment returns), benefiting from the **increased value** of the business, and having a **voice in managing** it.

### Sources of equity financing
Personal funds; family and friends; partners; venture capital firms; small business investment companies; angel investors; mergers and acquisitions (M&A); stock offerings; private placements; initial public offerings (IPOs).

- **Venture Capitalists (VCs):** invest in small enterprises with potential to thrive and disrupt the market; often have industry expertise; focused on the **return** from helping you grow rapidly. Notoriously hard to impress — the business's value must look set to soar and be relatively low-risk. Usually require you to **pitch** your plan; they "back horses about to win the Kentucky Derby" and say "no" often.
- **Angel Investors:** wealthy individuals who invest large sums for a healthy profit but **don't want to make decisions, provide contacts, or do work**. Suits owners who want to keep **full control** and just need funding — but the angel still wants a financial reward.
- **Family/Friends:** very common and accessible equity finance, but the **personal relationship is put at risk** and family/friends can be more imposing on your plans; the wider circle can be impacted if things go wrong.
- **Crowdfunding:** finance via loans, donations, or exchanging money for **rewards or shares**, usually through a crowdfunding website; you post your idea as a **campaign** and set a funding goal and deadline.
  - **7 steps:** (1) plan carefully (show what the product is, why to fund it, timeframe, incentives/rewards/shares); (2) set your goals (amount needed, timeframe, development time); (3) check legal responsibilities; (4) choose where to post; (5) post the campaign; (6) interact with your audience; (7) thank your backers.
- **Choosing an investor — selection criteria:** personal reasons, autonomy, needs of the business, needs of the investor.

### Revenue streams (revenue models)
- **Revenue stream:** the strategy a company uses to generate cash from each customer segment — revealing earnings from all the ways money comes in. Four main models:
  1. **Recurring** — predictable revenue at regular intervals (e.g., monthly phone plans). Harder to implement initially but more economical long-term.
  2. **Transaction-based** — revenue per transaction (e.g., buying a jacket). Less attractive than recurring because action is required to attract customers.
  3. **Project revenue** — from one-time projects; hard to predict; requires much effort to maintain customer relationships.
  4. **Service revenue** — the **least attractive** model because it essentially **sells time** rather than goods (e.g., consulting firms charging per hour).

### Generating revenue streams
- **Direct revenue stream** vs. **Ancillary revenue stream** (ancillary = secondary/subsidiary).
- **Upselling:** inducing a customer to buy more expensive items, upgrades, or add-ons.
- **Next selling:** following up after the sale with a similar product or exclusive offer.
- Each revenue stream may use different pricing tactics.

### The two key questions
(1) What are my revenue streams? (2) Within each stream, **how do I price the product?**

### Pricing
- **Pricing:** the process of setting the price at which a business sells its products/services; part of the marketing plan, a key variable in **microeconomic price allocation theory**, and one of the **four Ps** of the marketing mix (with product, promotion, place).
- **Micromarketing:** tailoring products, brands and promotions to microsegments; pricing customer/product combinations at store or individual level.
- **Dynamic pricing:** setting highly flexible prices based on changes in market demand.

### Economics of pricing (three factors)
- **Competition, Customer demand, and Costs.**
- **Competition factors:** price (sensitive to the economic climate), added value, convenience (location/hours), trust (testimonials), community membership.
- **Customer demand:**
  - **Demand** = quantity required and price people will pay; **demand curve** = units people would buy at different price levels.
  - **Price-elastic demand:** % change in quantity demanded for a 1% change in price.
  - **Price-inelastic demand:** % variation in sales is **less** than % variation in price.
  - **Factors influencing elasticity:** availability of substitutes, necessity of the product, share of the purchase in the consumer's total budget.
- **Costs:** **Fixed costs** (don't change with sales — rent, property taxes) vs. **Variable costs** (change in direct proportion to sales — sales commissions, labor).

### Psychological pricing
- Based on the theory that certain prices have a psychological impact; retail prices often expressed as **"odd prices"** just below a round number (e.g., $19.99).
- **Types:** **Prestige pricing** (for goods whose quality is hard to judge or with little info), **Odd pricing** ($9.99 vs. $10.00 to seem lower), **Reference pricing** (consumers have an idea of what the price should be).

### Dynamic & algorithmic pricing
- **Dynamic pricing** (a.k.a. surge/demand/time-based pricing): flexible prices based on current demand, set via algorithms considering competitor pricing, supply/demand and external factors. Common in hospitality, travel, entertainment, retail, electricity, public transport.
- **Algorithmic pricing:** automatically setting prices to **maximize profit**, using probabilistic/statistical info on buyers (Bayesian-optimal pricing) and competitor prices (e.g., auto-detect a competitor's lowest price and price within $1 of it).

### Common approaches to pricing
- **Cost-based:** Cost + markup; driven by internal economics, **not** customer insight; typically not strategic.
- **Value-based (value-optimized) pricing:** based on the buyer's **perceived value** (time saved, efficiency, luxury) rather than cost; most successful when products sell on emotion (fashion), in niche markets, in shortages (drinks at a festival on a hot day), or for indispensable add-ons (headsets). Improves profitability via higher prices without greatly reducing sales volume.

### Internal-oriented pricing strategies (formulas)
- **Cost-plus pricing** — **Markup** = amount added to cost:
  - Selling price = Cost + Markup
  - Markup = Selling price − Cost
  - Cost = Selling price − Markup
- **Target-return pricing** — set price to produce a given rate of return:
  - Target-return price = [(Fixed costs + target return) ÷ unit sales] + variable cost.

## Session 9 — Marketing, Advertising and HR

Three parts: **Marketing Plan & Strategies**, **Advertising Models**, and **Human Resource Management (HRM)**.

### Small business marketing
- Marketing is **far more than selling or advertising** — it's all activities needed to get a product from producer to consumer.
- **Peter Drucker:** a business has only two basic functions — **marketing and innovation** — the only efforts that produce results; everything else is a "cost."
- **Philip Kotler:** authentic marketing is not the art of selling what you make but **knowing what to make** — identifying and understanding customer needs and coming up with solutions that satisfy customers and produce profits.

### Marketing goals & objectives
- **Qualitative goals:** e.g., best customer service, perception of personalized service, highest quality, be the first business prospects think of.
- **Quantitative goals:** e.g., increase sales from $27K to $100K in 12 months, double customers in 2 years, +1 point market share in 18 months.
- **Objectives split into two groups:**
  - **Marketing-performance objectives:** specific, quantifiable outcomes (sales revenue, market share, profit) — e.g., "increase homeowner's insurance sales by 10% next fiscal year."
  - **Marketing-support objectives:** what must be accomplished first (educating customers, building awareness, creating image).

### The Marketing Mix — 4 Ps
- **Product:** position, name, packaging, product bundle, performance/features, service, warranty. *Why do customers buy?* — "people don't want a quarter-inch drill bit, they want a quarter-inch hole"; find the prospect's problem/"pain."
  - **Unique Selling Proposition (USP)** (Rosser Reeves, 1960): a specific, unique benefit that differentiates you (e.g., Domino's "fresh, hot pizza in 30 minutes," Volvo "safety," FedEx "when it has to be there overnight").
- **Price:** cost, competition, customer value, lease vs. sale, bundled, volume discount, current specials.
- **Promotion:** the average person sees ~4,000 marketing messages daily (Jack Trout). Methods: direct contact, networking & referral building, public speaking, writing & publicity, promotional events, advertising.
  - **Effective promotional tactics by stage:** create **Awareness** (news/press release, publicity, advertising, direct mail, open house, seminars, trade shows) → create **Comprehension** (personal selling, website, targeted advertising, workshops) → **Conviction** (personal sales, coupons, contests, rebates) → **Orders/Reorders** (trade shows, direct mail campaigns).
- **Place:** retail location, direct sales force, dealers/distributors/agents, volunteer "sales force."

### Lauterborn's 4 Cs (consumer-oriented counterpart to the 4 Ps)
- **Customer Solution** (satisfy the customer's needs, not just push a product) ↔ Product.
- **Cost** (total cost of using the product, incl. inconveniences and ethics — not just price) ↔ Price.
- **Convenience** (ease of purchase, geographic or online) ↔ Place.
- **Communication** (a lateral conversation between customer and business) ↔ Promotion.

### Marketing budget & schedule
- Track a monthly budget across items (website, networking, flyers, seminars, direct mail, etc.).
- **Promotional budget should be at least 1%–3% of gross sales.**

### Relationship marketing
- Business philosophy that broadens the marketing concept — concentrates on establishing a **long-term buyer-seller relationship** for the benefit of both parties.

### Developing a sales forecast
- The marketing plan should include a **sales forecast** predicting future sales in **dollars and units**. Two methods:
  - **Build-up method:** identify as many target markets as possible, predict sales for each, then combine (e.g., seniors' phones + smartphones + specialist phones).
  - **Break-down method:** start with total market potential for the product/industry, then break down into smaller units (e.g., if a trade association says 4% of the population is in the market at any time, estimate your realistic units/dollars). Ask whether enough of the target market is reachable to be profitable.
- **Sales & Operations Planning** (ERP context): forecast, production plan, inventory, working days, capacity, utilization.

### Identifying target markets
- **Market segmentation:** dividing the total market into identifiable groups (target markets) with a common want/need the business can satisfy, who can purchase and are more likely to buy.

### Advertising models
- **Advertising model:** strategic use of an advertising medium to reach a specific target audience. **Advertising medium** = the type of media/vehicle the ad is placed on.
- **Ehrenberg's ATRN model:** **Awareness** (let customers know you exist) → **Trial** (curiosity, not persuasion, leads to trial) → **Reinforcement** (reassurance in the brand — a good role for advertising) → **Nudging** (remind, reinforce, repeat purchase).
- **DRIP model:** **Differentiate** (be different from competition), **Reinforce** (consolidate/fortify the brand), **Inform** (what's new — features/benefits), **Persuade** (induce the customer to act positively).
- **AIDA model:** **Attention → Interest → Desire → Action.**
- **DAGMAR model** (Defining Advertising Goals for Measured Advertising Results; a.k.a. "Hierarchy of Effects"): from **Unawareness →** Stage 1 **Awareness →** Stage 2 **Comprehension →** Stage 3 **Conviction →** Stage 4 **Action** (purchase).
- **David Bernstein's VIPS checklist** for an effective ad message: **Visibility** (get it viewed; visual is best), **Identity** (strengthen brand/product image), **Promise** (gain/value/benefit to the customer), **Singlemindedness** (clarity, conciseness, focus).
- **Means-End Chain** (e.g., "Got Milk"): links product **features → benefits → values** (calcium → healthy bones → self-respect/wisdom).
- **Return on Attention (ROA):** ad-blocking software use is exploding (grew ~41% worldwide / 48% US between Q2 2014–Q2 2015; quadrupled 2013–2015 to 200M users). Three ROA components: (1) achieve more of what's valuable/meaningful per unit of time; (2) make time spent engaging/enjoyable; (3) reduce time spent unless it adds significant value.
- **Innovative marketing:** QR codes; retina/eye-tracking technology (heat maps of where consumers look).
- **Lessons learnt:** no one knows for sure why some ads work and others don't; models should **guide creativity, not act as a "straitjacket."**

### Human Resource Management (HRM)
- Hiring is one of the most important and difficult tasks for a small business owner — especially in a service business, a **bad hire could cause the business to fail**. "A small business's most valuable assets walk out the door each day at closing time."
- **Job analysis:** gathering all information about a job (what is done, how, by whom, to what degree) — the **foundation on which all other HR activities are based**; includes the job description and job specifications.
- **Job/Position description:** identifies the duties, tasks and responsibilities of a position (no standard format exists).
- **Job specifications / selection criteria:** the skills, abilities, knowledge, experience and personal requirements needed; applicants address these in their application.
- **Interviewing:** viewed by employers as the **most critical step** in selection; face-to-face is more prominent than phone; ask **behavioural** and other questions.
- **Fair Work Australia / Fair Work Commission:** the regulator governing employment (relevant to hiring and workplace conditions).

## Session 10 — Share Market: How Small Business Can Expand Their Funds

### Why invest?
- **Investing for capital growth:** capital growth = the value of the investment increases; most people invest to build wealth over time and protect against **inflation**.
- **Investing for income:** an income stream to supplement main income (e.g., retirees living off **dividends** from shares and **rent** from property; **fixed-interest products** like bonds and hybrids offer regular income).

### How to invest — draw up a plan
- **Financial planning:** analyse your current financial position; identify the level of **risk** you're comfortable with; consider your stage of life and **investment time frame**; regularly review your portfolio.
- **Investment strategy:** **diversification** ("don't put all your eggs in one basket") and an objective, consistent approach.

### Four main investment areas
1. **Cash** (e.g., business). 2. **Fixed interest** (bank term deposits or bonds). 3. **Property** (real estate). 4. **Shares** (the focus of this session).

### What is a share?
- A **share** = a single unit of **equity ownership** in a company. A company raises money by **"going public"** — being listed on a stock exchange and issuing shares. By paying for shares, an investor buys part ownership and becomes a **shareholder**.

### Share categories
- **Income shares:** pay larger dividends (for income without selling); price generally doesn't rise quickly.
- **Blue chip shares:** issued by companies with long histories of growth/stability; pay regular dividends; steady price trend.
- **Growth shares:** issued by fast-growing entrepreneurial companies; pay little/no dividends (earnings reinvested for expansion).
- **Cyclical shares:** affected by economic trends — fall in recession, rise in booms (e.g., mining, heavy machinery, home building).
- **Defensive shares:** opposite of cyclical; producers of staples (food, beverages, pharmaceuticals, insurance); maintain value during downturns.

### What is the share market?
- Provides **capital-raising services and trading facilities**. Most industrialised countries have one (LSE, NYSE, Tokyo). The **Australian Securities Exchange (ASX)** is a multi-asset-class exchange and one of the world's top-10 listed exchange groups by market capitalisation.
- **Two market functions:**
  - **Primary market:** companies raise money by **issuing shares** to investors — the process of offering new shares to the public is a **float** or **Initial Public Offering (IPO)**. A **prospectus** (formal document) is lodged with **ASIC** for verification.
  - **Secondary market:** investors **buy and sell** existing shares at prices set by market forces; shares can only be traded on the ASX **through a stockbroker**.

### Stockbrokers
- **Stockbroker:** a financial professional who buys and sells stocks at clients' direction; acts as your agent — shares cannot be traded directly with the ASX.
- **Full-service (advisory) broker:** provides advice, investment recommendations, research, and tailored investment planning.
- **Non-advisory (discount) broker:** "execution only" — no advice; operates by phone/internet; **lower fees**.

### Shareholders — rights and benefits
- Profit by selling shares for more than paid. Rights/benefits: participate in **annual general meetings**; receive reports/information; **dividends** (a % of earnings paid as profit) and **dividend reinvestment plans (DRIP/DRP)**; further share issues; **share buy-backs** (company buys its shares back); other corporate actions.

### Risks of shares
- **Capital loss risk** (company fails), **volatility risk** (rapid price swings), **timing risk** (higher when the market has risen sharply), **poor-quality advice risk**, **legislative risk** (law/tax changes), **currency risk** (adverse currency moves on overseas shares).
- **Best time to buy?** A share's value can fall to zero. Even Warren Buffett can't predict short-term movements — but he knows there will be rises/falls, and that **short-term volatility doesn't matter as long as it rises over the medium-to-long term**.

### Benefits of shares
- Shares for **capital growth**; shares for **dividend income**; and the **dividend reinvestment plan (DRIP/DRP)** — reinvest dividends into more shares instead of taking the cash.

### Trading, settling and CHESS
- **Trading:** orders placed with a broker (phone/internet) typically enter the **Central Limit Order Book (CLOB)**, where buy/sell orders are matched by price in the order entered.
- **Settlement:** exchanging title/legal ownership of shares for money. On the ASX this occurs via the **CHESS** system (**Clearing House Electronic Subregister System**) — fully computerised, so **paper certificates are no longer used**.
- **Two ways shares are registered:**
  1. **CHESS / Broker Sponsored** — tracked with a **Holder Identification Number (HIN)** (shares managed by a broker).
  2. **Issuer Sponsored Subregister** — tracked with a **Shareholder Reference Number (SRN)** (shares held by the company's share registry).

### Market indices and sectors
- **Index:** a measure of performance over time; a **sharemarket index** measures the price change of a **basket of shares** (the more shares, the closer it represents the whole market) — e.g., the **Dow Jones Index** (top NYSE companies).
- **Market sectors** (2,000+ ASX companies can be ordered by):
  - **Capitalisation:** large-cap ($10 billion+) vs. small-cap ($250 million–$2 billion).
  - Historical performance and price volatility.
  - **Industry divide:** **resources** companies (exploration/mining of minerals, oil, gas) vs. **industrials** (all others).
- **GICS (Global Industry Classification Standard):** assigns companies to sector/industry group/industry/sub-industry across **four levels** (as of 2021: 11 sectors, 24 industry groups, 69 industries, 158 sub-industries).

### Technical analysis
- **Technical analysis:** study of past price movements of a share or the market. **Charts** are the key tool: **line charts**, **bar charts** (open, high, low, close over a period), and **candlestick charts** (blank/green for up days, coloured/red for down days).
- **Trend analysis:**
  - **Uptrend:** each subsequent high is higher than the previous high, and each subsequent low is higher than the previous low. **Downtrend** is the opposite.
  - **Resistance and support lines** define buying/selling points: the market **resists** rising when sellers enter at a price; price is **supported** from falling by buyers at a price. **Rising volume** at a break of support/resistance confirms the break is legitimate.
  - **Moving Average:** levels out price data over a specified period via a constantly updated average price.
